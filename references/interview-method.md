# Interview Method

The difference between an interview and a chat is **preparation and purpose**. Every question should be doing a job. Before you ask, you've done your homework (Phase 1) and read the author profile (Phase 0), so your questions are specific to *this* topic and *this* author, not generic prompts.

**Ask in the author's language** (set in `profile/voice.md`). Technical terms can stay in their original form inline. Match the author's register; the example questions below are a guide to *kind*, not wording.

## Question types

Aim for a mix. A good 3 to 5 question interview usually pulls from several of these:

- **Excavate experience.** Get the concrete story only this author can tell. "Take me back to the moment you realized X. What actually happened?" "What did you try first, and why didn't it work?" Specifics are what make writing credible and unfakeable.
- **Challenge the assumption.** Pressure-test a claim. "You're assuming X here. What has to be true for that to hold?" "What's the strongest argument against your own position?"
- **Force the distinction.** Make a fuzzy idea sharp. "How is this actually different from [the obvious adjacent concept]?" "If you could keep only one of these points, which one?"
- **Push to reflection.** Get the larger meaning. "Does this change the advice you'd give someone? How?" "What's something you didn't believe a year ago and believe now?"
- **Find the tension.** Surface a contradiction the author may not have noticed, including against their own past positions (read the adjacent pieces via `profile/work.md`).

## Pacing

- Ask **one or two questions at a time.** A wall of questions gets shallow answers. The interview is a conversation, not a form.
- Lead with the question most likely to unlock the author's energy, usually an *excavate-experience* question. People warm up by telling a story, not by defending a thesis.
- Plan for **3 to 5 primary questions**, plus follow-ups. Past about 6 rounds the author tires; the draft shouldn't depend on exhaustive coverage.

## Follow-up logic: where the interview earns its keep

After each answer, run a quick check. Ask **one** sharp follow-up if you see:

- **A thin answer**, one or two sentences where there's clearly more. "Say more about ___, what did that look like concretely?"
- **A buzzword or abstraction.** "When you say 'scalable', what specifically do you mean here?"
- **An untested claim.** "What's the evidence? Did this actually happen to you?"
- **A hidden contradiction**, with itself or with a past article. Name it gently and let the author resolve it.
- **An unexpected thread.** Sometimes the best material is a throwaway aside. Pull on it.

A model can reliably ask *competent, structured* questions. The thing it's weaker at is the *surprising* follow-up, catching the contradiction the author didn't see. Closing that gap is the whole point of this step, so spend your attention here. When in doubt, the follow-up that names a tension ("these two points seem to pull against each other, how do you reconcile them?") is usually the valuable one.

## Honor the author's voice in the questions

Match the author's language and register (from `profile/voice.md`). Don't interrogate; converse. The author should feel *understood and pushed*, not *processed*.

## Cold start: new or thin profile

If `profile/author.md` or `profile/work.md` is mostly empty, fold a little profiling into the interview, lightly, without making it feel like onboarding:
- One question early that reveals both stance and expertise (an excavate-experience question usually does both).
- Note the author's natural vocabulary, sentence length, and the references they reach for; that *is* their voice. Record it afterward in `profile/author.md` or `profile/voice.md`.
- Ask where their existing writing lives (a folder, a blog, a notes app) and add it to `profile/work.md`. Real past writing is the best voice source there is.
- Each session makes the next one sharper. The profile is the compounding asset, so always invest a little in filling it.

## When to stop

Stop interviewing when you can answer, for the planned article: *what is the one thing this says, and what are the three or four supporting moves?*, and every one of them traces to an answer. If you can't, you're missing a question, not a paragraph.
